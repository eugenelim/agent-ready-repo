# Grounding copy goals in stable referents

After the interrogation converges on named copy goals, each goal must be grounded in at least one **stable referent** before it can steer a copy decision on this surface. A referent is stable when it exists outside the current session and can be pointed to — so a later copy choice traces back to it, not to a fresh opinion.

## The three referent types

### 1 — Persona language

Grounding a copy goal in persona language means grounding it in the words the audience actually uses — their vocabulary, their framing, their idioms — mined from real interactions with this audience type. This is the strongest referent because it connects the goal directly to the reader's frame of reference.

**Sources for persona language:**
- Support ticket transcripts — the words customers use when something is wrong; their language under friction is unfiltered.
- Sales call recordings or notes — how prospects describe the problem they are trying to solve, before any product vocabulary has landed.
- Community posts or forums — the phrasing people use when they talk about the problem among themselves, without performing for a brand.
- Survey verbatims — open-ended responses that capture the reader's own language, not the survey's vocabulary.

If VoC data is absent, elicit persona language inline: ask "What words does your audience use when they describe this problem to a colleague?" Record the answer as a sketch — it is an approximation, not a validated referent — and flag the resulting goals as **directional — not backed by VoC research** until real VoC data is available.

A copy goal grounded only in the team's sense of what sounds right is a preference, not a referent. Push it back to elicitation.

### 2 — Copy precedents

A copy precedent is a named example of copy that carries the quality the goal names. Precedents are quality anchors: they demonstrate that the quality is achievable and provide a concrete pointer the team can orient to without abstracting the goal into a formula.

**How to use a precedent:**
Name the precedent, name the specific attribute you are taking from it, and name what you are leaving. For example: "Stripe's landing page — the clarity of the claim structure (value before features) — not the premium register, which is too cold for our audience." The named attribute is the referent; the whole example is not.

**What precedents are not:**
- Templates. Never say "write copy like Stripe." Extract which qualities ground the goal; the goal then stands on its own.
- Licenses. A precedent shows the quality is achievable; it does not mean the copy should sound like the precedent.

Common copy precedent domains for marketing/acquisition surfaces:
- B2B SaaS landing pages — high-clarity claim structures, painkiller framing, guide-not-guru register
- Developer tool marketing — precision, example-forward, no marketing register inflation
- Consumer product marketing — aspirational without vagueness, identity-aligned, permission to be informal
- Challenger brands — conviction-led copywriting, the claim before the caveat

### 3 — Persuasion standards

Persuasion standards are named frameworks or methods for evaluating whether copy works on this surface. Cite the standard by name and apply it functionally — name what the standard says to do, not what it contains.

**Painkiller-first framing.** The reader's problem before the product's features. A copy direction that leads with the product ("We built X for Y") before the problem ("Y teams struggle with Z") buries the entry point for a reader who has not yet confirmed they have the problem. A copy goal of "Problem-first voice" is grounded when it names painkiller-first framing as the standard it respects.

**Tweet test.** Can the headline stand alone as a conviction statement in 280 characters or fewer? If the headline needs the subheadline to be coherent, it is failing the tweet test — the core claim is not yet compressed enough to travel. A copy goal of "Self-contained clarity" is grounded when it names the tweet test as its verification standard.

**Five-second evaluator scan.** A skeptical reader scanning above-fold copy for five seconds should be able to answer: is this for me, and what is the claim? If they cannot, the copy is not doing its job at the register the goal requires. A copy goal of "Immediate relevance" is grounded when it names the five-second scan as its verification standard.

## Recording the grounding

For each named copy goal in the copy-direction doc, record:

- **Persona language** — the words the audience actually uses that ground this goal, or a note that VoC is absent and the referent is directional.
- **Copy precedent** — one or two named examples that carry the quality, and which specific attribute you are taking from each.
- **Persuasion standard** — the named standard the goal respects (painkiller-first, tweet test, five-second scan).

A goal that clears all three is well-grounded. A goal that clears one or two can proceed with the missing referents recorded as open questions. A goal that clears none goes back to Stage 2 of the interrogation.
