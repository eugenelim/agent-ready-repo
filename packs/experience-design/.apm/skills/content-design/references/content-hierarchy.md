# Content hierarchy

The Nava PBC text-prototype model provides a practical prioritisation method for product and reference surfaces. It prevents the most common product-content failure mode: pages where the least-needed information is most prominent because it was most interesting to write, while the must-have information is buried or absent.

## The three tiers

**Must-say** — content the surface cannot exist without. The reader cannot accomplish their task or make their decision without it. If a must-say item is absent, the surface fails its primary job. Must-say items belong in the main content path: above the fold for acquisition surfaces, in the opening paragraph or first section for product/reference surfaces. Test: "If this is the only sentence the reader reads, can they still proceed?" If yes, it is must-say.

**Probably-say** — content that helps most readers but is not the minimum viable answer. It expands the must-say with context, caveats, and examples that make the primary content more useful. Probably-say items belong in the body — reachable within one scroll or one section, but not competing with must-say for the opening position. Test: "Would most readers regret not seeing this?" If yes, it is probably-say.

**Might-say** — content that serves edge cases, advanced readers, or the minority who need the full detail. It should be present but not in the reader's primary path. Might-say items belong in expandable sections, footnotes, linked reference pages, or end-of-page appendices. Test: "What percentage of readers need this?" If the answer is a small minority, it is might-say.

## Applying the model

For each content item identified during elicitation, assign a tier. Do this before writing any copy — the assignment is a constraint that shapes where each item appears, not a label added afterward.

Common mis-assignments to watch for:

- **Metadata promoted to must-say.** Version numbers, last-updated dates, and author attribution are almost always might-say; they serve a small subset of readers who need provenance. Promoting them above the must-say content buries the answer.
- **Context demoted to might-say.** A one-sentence explanation of why a step exists is often must-say — without it, readers skip the step and call it wrong. The context feels like overhead until a reader follows the wrong path because it was absent.
- **Caveats treated as must-say.** "This only works if X" is must-say when X is a common case; it is might-say when X is a rare configuration. Caveat promotion makes surfaces feel hostile — everything is a warning before the answer.

## Application to scroll section sequencing

For acquisition surfaces, the must-say → probably-say → might-say model applies to scroll section ordering as well as within-section content. The must-say section (the one the reader cannot leave without understanding) belongs above the fold or immediately below it. Probably-say sections belong in the body. Might-say sections — detailed technical specs, extensive social proof lists, comprehensive FAQ — belong at the bottom or in a linked detail page.

This is distinct from the narrative arc (which governs the *argument* the content makes) — a StoryBrand arc still observes content hierarchy within each element: the must-say version of the Problem section is a single resonant sentence; the probably-say version adds the internal and philosophical dimensions; the might-say version adds data and examples. The arc shapes the sequence; the hierarchy shapes the depth.
