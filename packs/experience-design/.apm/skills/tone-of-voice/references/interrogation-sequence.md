# The copy interrogation sequence

How to turn a felt copy vibe into a short set of named copy goals. Run it as a conversation, not a form. Each stage narrows: a register sense becomes attributes, attributes become a few goals you can rank and arbitrate.

## Stage 1 — Open from the felt register

Start where the user already is. Ask for the feeling of the copy in their words before reaching for any craft vocabulary:

- "What three words would describe this brand's copy at its best?"
- "When someone reads our headline, what should they feel in the first three seconds?"
- "What would the corporate-bad version of our copy sound like? What exactly makes it wrong for us?"

Capture the raw words verbatim. "Direct but warm," "expert but not arrogant," "like a colleague, not a manual" — these are the seeds. Do not translate them yet.

## Stage 2 — Probe the register and associations

For each seed word or phrase, find what is underneath it. The same register word means different things to different teams; the job is to pin down *this* meaning.

- **Register distinction** — "When you say 'direct,' is that direct as in blunt, or direct as in no-nonsense-but-kind? What would the over-direct version get wrong?" Two readings of the same word point at two different directions.
- **Association** — "What copy would you point to as carrying this quality? A product, an author, a newsletter — anything where the tone is right?" Associations carry a bundle of qualities the user feels but cannot name; the interrogation unbundles them in Stage 3.
- **Failure mode** — "What would the over-friendly version sound like? What would the over-expert version sound like?" The failure modes on both sides of the goal are often easier to name than the goal itself; they triangulate the target.

## Stage 3 — Converge on named copy goals

From the probed words, draft a short set of goals — three to five, not ten. Each goal is a **noun phrase a non-designer can recall a week later**:

- Good: "Earned authority," "Warm directness," "Guide-not-guru," "Peer-level confidence."
- Too vague to arbitrate: "Professional," "friendly," "clear," "engaging." These describe nothing a specific copy choice can be measured against — push them back into Stage 2 ("friendly *like a colleague* or *like a salesperson*?") until a recallable phrase falls out.

Fewer, sharper goals beat more, fuzzier ones. If you have eight candidates, the user is describing several brands; ask which is *this* one.

## Stage 4 — Sharpen each goal against its opposite

A copy goal you cannot violate is not a goal — it is a platitude. For each named goal, state what would break it and what the goal is choosing *over*:

- "Earned authority" — broken by claims the copy cannot back up, or by expertise shown rather than earned. Choosing this *over* "confident assertiveness," which would let us lead with the claim before the proof.
- "Warm directness" — broken by either clinical distance (too direct) or softening that buries the point (too warm). Choosing this *over* "approachable warmth," which would let us hedge when we should just say the thing.

The opposite is the test. If nothing plausible would violate the goal, it is too soft to steer by — rewrite it until a real copy choice could fail it.

## Handling "make it sound like X" (example-only users)

Some users can only point: "make our copy feel like Notion's" or "like Stripe's landing page." That is useful raw material, not a direction — copying X whole gives you X's direction, not yours. Unbundle the example:

1. **Name the qualities you are after.** "What specifically about their copy is right for us — the brevity? the plainness? the lack of jargon? the claim structure?" Pull two or three qualities out of the example and treat those as Stage 2 attributes.
2. **Name what you are leaving.** "What about X is wrong for us?" The rejected qualities are as load-bearing as the chosen ones — they prevent cargo-culting the full reference.
3. **Re-derive as goals.** Feed the kept qualities back through Stage 3 so the direction stands on its own named goals, with X demoted to "one reference among several," not the spec.

If a user offers several examples, the overlap between them is the signal — the qualities all references share are the ones the user actually feels. Name those.

## Exit

The interrogation is done when you have three to five named copy goals, each with its violation test, ready to ground in referents (Step 3) and rank (Step 4). Goals that survived their opposite are real; goals that did not go back to Stage 2.
