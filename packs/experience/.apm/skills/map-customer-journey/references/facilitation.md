# Facilitating the mapping workshop

Journeys, blueprints, and process maps are most often built *with* people, not
for them — in a facilitated session with the customers, operators, and
stakeholders who hold the knowledge. This is the pack's shared facilitation
method: the connective skills (`map-customer-journey`, `map-internal-process`,
and the workshop steps of `map-screen-flow` / `blueprint-service`) point here
when the map is elicited collaboratively. It is method, not a tool — name a
*shared canvas* (a virtual whiteboard, or wall space and sticky notes), never a
product.

## A workshop is a decision milestone, not a discussion

Run the session to *produce* something — a named deliverable, agreed by the
people in the room — not to talk. Three things make that happen:

- **A stated purpose** (two or three sentences) and a **tangible deliverable**
  (the journey map, the as-is/to-be process, the prioritized pains).
- **The right people, kept few.** Invite diverse perspectives, but keep the
  group small enough that everyone contributes — past a handful, the quiet stop
  talking and the session becomes a presentation.
- **A planned shape**, designed backwards from the deliverable (below).

## Design the agenda backwards

Start from the deliverable and work back to the opening:

1. **Deliverable** — what leaves the room.
2. **Activities** — chunk the time: a short intro, context-setting, the main
   mapping activities, a synthesis block, and explicit next steps.
3. **Buffer** — add a generous time cushion over your estimate; sessions always
   run long, and a rushed synthesis wastes the whole workshop.

## Diverge before you converge

The single most useful rule: **generate before you evaluate, alone before
together.**

- **Silent, individual generation first.** Have everyone write their own
  observations (on their own notes) *before* anyone speaks. This surfaces more
  ideas, and it defuses the two failure modes of group work — the dominant voice
  who anchors the room, and the quiet expert who never gets the floor.
- **Quantity before quality.** In the divergent phase, chase volume; a "wrong"
  observation often sparks the right one. Judge later.
- **Then converge** with a lightweight decision method: cluster related notes by
  affinity, let people vote with a fixed number of dots to surface priorities,
  and place candidates on an impact-vs-effort grid to separate the quick wins
  from the major bets.

## Keep everyone in, and keep on track

- **Engage the quiet:** think → pair → share (reflect alone, compare in a pair,
  then bring it to the group); a direct, named invitation ("what's your read on
  this step?"); a low-pressure warm-up to start.
- **Contain the dominant:** lead with the silent write, time-box each person's
  share, rotate who speaks first.
- **Park, don't pursue.** When something off-topic but important comes up, write
  it on a visible "parking lot" and return to it later; summarize after each
  block; take a break every hour or so.

## The journey-mapping activity (and its inside-out sibling)

For a **customer journey**: anchor on a **single persona** so the map stays
coherent; lay out the journey **phases**; mark the **touchpoints** at each
(digital and physical); have the group **color-code the emotional state** along
the path; then cluster the **pains** where the emotion turns negative and trace
each to a root cause — those become the opportunities. (This is exactly the
stages × actions / emotions / pains / opportunities the skill records.)

For an **internal process** (`map-internal-process`): the same facilitation
spine, with the operators rather than customers in the room — a process
walkthrough as the default activity, a multi-stakeholder session when the
process crosses departments, and a go-and-see observation for a
high-exception process. The deliverable is the as-is map, its pain/waste
register, and the agreed to-be.

## Remote vs. in-person

Keep everyone in the **same mode** — all-remote or all-in-person; a mixed room
silences the remote half. Remote: a shared virtual canvas as the single source
of truth, everyone visible, small-group breakouts for divergent work,
quick-reaction voting. In-person: open wall space, sticky notes, markers.
Either way, **pre-populate the templates** (the empty journey grid, the
impact/effort matrix) so the group spends its time thinking, not formatting, and
time-box each activity visibly but without a stressful countdown.

## Before and after

- **Before:** send prep materials ahead; assign roles (a facilitator who never
  also takes notes, a dedicated note-taker, section owners); test the tooling
  early.
- **During:** capture *every* contribution, not just the ones you like; record
  each decision and name an owner for each action item.
- **After:** send a recap quickly, while momentum holds — the artifact, the
  decisions, and the action items with owners and dates. The map is only as
  durable as the follow-through.
