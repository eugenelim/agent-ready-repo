# The interrogation sequence

How to turn a felt vibe into a short set of named goals. Run it as a
conversation, not a form. Each stage narrows: a mood becomes emotions,
emotions become attributes, attributes become a few goals you can rank.

## Stage 1 — Open from the felt word

Start where the user already is. Ask for the feeling in their words before
you reach for any design vocabulary:

- "When this works, how does the user feel in the first three seconds?"
- "Describe it like a person walking into a room — what's the room like?"
- "What feeling are we trying *not* to leave them with?"

Capture the raw words verbatim. "Calm," "serious money," "fun but not
childish" — these are the seeds. Don't translate them yet.

## Stage 2 — Probe the emotions and associations

For each seed word, find what's underneath it. The same word means
different things to different people; your job is to pin down *this*
meaning.

- **Emotion** — "When you say calm, is that *relaxed* calm, or *in-control*
  calm? Spa, or cockpit?" Two readings of one word point at two different
  directions.
- **Association** — "What does this remind you of — a brand, a place, an
  object?" Associations carry a bundle of qualities the user feels but
  can't name; you'll unbundle them in Stage 4.
- **Brand attribute** — "If the product were a person, what three adjectives
  would a friend use? What would an enemy use?" The enemy's words tell you
  the failure mode the direction must avoid.

## Stage 3 — Converge on named goals

From the probed words, draft a **short set** of goals — three to five, not
ten. Each goal is a **noun phrase a non-designer can recall a week later**:

- Good: "Quiet confidence," "Effortless first run," "Premium without
  flash."
- Too vague to arbitrate: "Modern," "clean," "nice," "professional." These
  describe nothing a choice can be measured against — push them back into
  Stage 2 ("modern *like what?* what would make it feel dated?") until a
  recallable phrase falls out.

Fewer, sharper goals beat more, fuzzier ones. If you have eight candidates,
the user is describing several products; ask which is *this* one.

## Stage 4 — Sharpen each goal against its opposite

A goal you can't violate isn't a goal — it's a platitude. For each named
goal, state what would **break** it, and what the goal is choosing *over*:

- "Quiet confidence" — broken by loud, attention-grabbing flourishes;
  chosen over "exciting," which we are deliberately not.
- "Effortless first run" — broken by anything that asks the user to decide
  before they've seen value; chosen over "powerful and configurable
  up front."

The opposite is the test. If nothing plausible would violate the goal, it's
too soft to steer by — rewrite it until a real choice could fail it.

## Handling "make it feel like X" (example-only users)

Some users can only point: "make it feel like that other app." That's
useful raw material, not a direction — copying X whole gives you X's
direction, not yours, and you can't defend a choice with "because X did
it." Unbundle the example:

1. **Name the qualities you're after.** "What about X are we chasing — the
   restraint? the density? the warmth?" Pull two or three qualities out of
   the example and treat *those* as Stage-2 attributes.
2. **Name what you're leaving.** "What about X is wrong for us?" The
   rejected qualities are as load-bearing as the chosen ones — they keep
   the team from cargo-culting the whole reference.
3. **Re-derive as goals.** Feed the kept qualities back through Stage 3 so
   the direction stands on its own named goals, with X demoted to "one
   reference among several," not the spec.

If a user offers several examples, the *overlap* between them is the
signal — the qualities all the references share are the ones the user
actually feels. Name those.

## Exit

You're done when you have three-to-five named goals, each with its
violation test, ready to rank in `coherence-arbitration.md`. Goals that
survived their opposite are real; goals that didn't go back to Stage 2.
