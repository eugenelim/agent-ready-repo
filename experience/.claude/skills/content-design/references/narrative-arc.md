# Narrative arc selection

Three named narrative arc frameworks cover the two surface types: StoryBrand and Conversion-Centered Design for acquisition surfaces (arc selection driven by audience awareness level); the Pyramid Principle for analytical and structured product/reference surfaces (arc selection driven by reader action goal and prior knowledge level). Name the selected arc and state the applicability rationale before writing section assignments. An arc selected without a rationale is a guess.

## Acquisition arcs (awareness-level driven)

### StoryBrand — seven-element arc

Used for cold and warm audiences: Schwartz awareness levels 1 (Unaware), 2 (Problem-Aware), and 3 (Solution-Aware). Readers at these levels do not yet have enough context to evaluate a product on its merits; they need a narrative that places them in a story they recognise before they can engage with the offer.

The arc has seven elements: a **Character** (the reader, not the company), a **Problem** the character faces (external — the situation; internal — how it makes them feel; philosophical — why it matters), a **Guide** (the company or product, positioned as the one who helps), a **Plan** (the concrete, low-risk path from problem to success), a **CTA** (the direct ask), **Stakes** (what is at stake if nothing changes), and a **Success** picture (what life looks like after the problem is solved). The elements do not need to appear in this order on every surface — scroll sections may be reordered to suit the reader's journey — but every element should be findable somewhere on the surface.

The key applicability test: if the reader does not yet recognise their own problem in your first sentence, StoryBrand's problem-first opening is correct. Start with what the reader feels, not what the product does.

### Conversion-Centered Design — seven principles

Used for bottom-of-funnel audiences: Schwartz awareness levels 4 (Product-Aware) and 5 (Most Aware). Readers at these levels already know the product; the arc's job is to remove friction and make the conversion obvious, not to tell a story.

The seven principles — Attention (direct focus to one thing), Context (give the reader exactly what they need at this moment), Clarity (remove ambiguity about the offer), Congruence (every element supports the conversion goal), Credibility (proof that the claim is true), Closing (the right ask at the right moment), and Continuance (what happens after the conversion, removing post-click anxiety) — are applied as a checklist against the existing content structure rather than as a sequential narrative.

The key applicability test: if the reader already knows what they want to do and the page's job is to make doing it obvious and trustworthy, CCD is correct. Complexity is friction at this level; the arc's job is subtraction, not addition.

## Product/reference arc (action-goal driven)

### Pyramid Principle — conclusion-first structure

Used for analytical and structured product/reference surfaces where the reader's primary action goal is **Decision** (they need to evaluate options or commit to a path) or **Understanding** (they need a concept explained before they can act) and they arrive with high prior knowledge of the domain. The Pyramid Principle organises content with the conclusion stated first, supported top-down: the main point is given immediately, then the supporting reasoning, then the detail. This structure respects the reader's cognitive priority at high prior knowledge levels — they want the answer before the evidence, not the problem before the solution.

Four structural principles:
- **Conclusion first** — state the main point or recommendation before any supporting argument; the reader can stop reading when they have what they need.
- **Top-down hierarchy** — each level answers the question raised by the level above; no level introduces a concept the level above did not raise.
- **Logical clustering** — parallel items at the same level share the same relationship to their parent (all reasons, all steps, all options — never mixed).
- **Structured progression** — within a cluster, order items from most important to least, or from simplest to most complex, depending on the reader's task.

The key applicability test: does the reader arrive already knowing why the topic matters and wanting the answer directly? If yes, the Pyramid Principle is the right structure. It is the wrong structure for cold-audience acquisition surfaces (where the problem must be established before the conclusion can land) and for readers with low prior knowledge (who need the context layer before the conclusion makes sense).

## Applicability rationale requirement

Before selecting an arc, state in one sentence why it is the right choice: "CCD because the reader is Product-Aware — they already know they want a trial; the surface's job is to remove friction from the sign-up path" or "Pyramid Principle because this is a decision guide for experienced users choosing between two integration paths — they need the recommendation first, then the supporting comparison." A rationale that disagrees with the awareness level (for acquisition) or the action goal and prior knowledge level (for product/reference) is a signal the inputs were diagnosed incorrectly.

## When awareness level is uncertain

If the team does not know the reader's awareness level, do not default to one arc — default to the question. Ask: "What does the reader know about the problem before they arrive?" If they know nothing, they are Unaware or Problem-Aware; StoryBrand is almost always correct. If they arrived via a specific branded search or a product CTA, they are Product-Aware; CCD is almost always correct.

Assuming a higher awareness level than the reader actually has is the more common failure mode — it produces content that assumes conviction the reader has not yet formed. When uncertain, write for the lower awareness level; a Most Aware reader can skim a StoryBrand arc without friction, but an Unaware reader cannot parse a CCD offer without context.
