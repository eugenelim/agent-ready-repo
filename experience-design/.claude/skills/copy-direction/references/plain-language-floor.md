# Plain-language floor

Before a copy direction is closed, it must clear a plain-language floor — a set of minimum standards that ensure the direction does not systematically exclude readers or create copy that fails in translation. The floor is not a negotiable trade-off against a copy goal; it applies regardless of direction.

## Governing standards

Two bodies of guidance define the floor:

**GOV.UK Content Design guidance** — the plain-language and content design principles developed by the UK Government Digital Service. Write for the reading level of the broadest relevant audience; use the word your reader uses, not the word that sounds correct; structure content around what the user needs to do, not around how the organisation is structured. Named here as the plain-language standard for digital product and service content.

**US Federal Plain Language Guidelines** — the PLAIN Act guidelines maintained by plainlanguage.gov. Use common, everyday words; write in active voice; use short sentences; design for the user's task. Named here as the plain-language standard for document, reference, and public-facing content.

Neither standard is reprinted here. They are named as stable references the direction must respect.

## The three step-7 checks

**Check 1 — No jargon the reader did not bring.** Review each named copy goal against the reader map from Step 1. For each goal, ask: "Does achieving this goal require the reader to know terminology they may not have arrived with?" If a goal's natural expression introduces specialist terms, the goal must either be grounded in a referent that translates the jargon (persona language that uses it natively) or restated in terms the reader brings.

**Check 2 — No idioms that do not translate.** Review each named copy goal for idioms or culturally-local figures of speech that may not carry meaning for readers from different regions or language backgrounds. Flag idiom-dependent goals as open questions if the reader population is not known to be culturally homogeneous.

**Check 3 — No assumptions about the reader's identity.** Review the reader map and each named copy goal for embedded assumptions about the reader's background, expertise level, gender, age, or circumstances that are not grounded in explicit audience data. Assumptions surfaced here are recorded as open questions, not overridden — the goal may be correct, but the assumption must be named explicitly.

## Recording floor notes

If any check surfaces a tension with a named copy goal, record it in the copy-direction doc's "Plain-Language Floor Notes" section:

- Name the goal that pulled against the floor.
- Name which check it failed and how.
- Record the resolution: either how the goal was restated to clear the floor, or why the goal is correct and the floor concern is addressed (with the specific evidence).

Floor notes that cannot be resolved during this session are recorded as open questions, not omitted.
